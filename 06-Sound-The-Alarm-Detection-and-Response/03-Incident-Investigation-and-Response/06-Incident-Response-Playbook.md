## üß™ Real-World Example: DDoS Detection Playbook

### Scenario
An organization notices **unusual inbound traffic spikes**.

### Example Playbook Flow (Non-Automated)

1. Identify Indicators of Compromise (IoCs)
   - Unknown IPs
   - Traffic spikes
2. Collect Logs
   - Firewall logs
   - IDS alerts
3. Analyze Evidence
   - Traffic volume
   - Source patterns
4. Determine Severity
5. Escalate or Mitigate

üìå This flow is often represented as a **flowchart** for clarity.

## üß© Types of Playbooks

There are **three types** of incident response playbooks:

---

### 1Ô∏è‚É£ Non-Automated Playbooks

**Definition:**  
Fully manual, analyst-driven, step-by-step guides.

**Used when:**
- Human judgment is required
- Incident is complex or unique
- Automation is risky

**Example:**
- Initial investigation of a suspected insider threat

üìå Best for training and foundational response.

---

### 2Ô∏è‚É£ Automated Playbooks

**Definition:**  
Tasks are executed automatically using tools.

**Common automated actions:**
- Alert severity classification
- Evidence collection
- IP blocking
- Ticket creation

**Tools involved:**
- SIEM
- SOAR

üìå Automation reduces **time to resolution (TTR)** significantly.

---

### 3Ô∏è‚É£ Semi-Automated Playbooks (Most Common)

**Definition:**  
Combination of **automation + human decision-making**.

**What gets automated:**
- Repetitive
- Time-consuming
- Error-prone tasks

**What humans handle:**
- Decision-making
- Context analysis
- Escalation judgment

üìå This is the **most effective and realistic model** in SOCs.
