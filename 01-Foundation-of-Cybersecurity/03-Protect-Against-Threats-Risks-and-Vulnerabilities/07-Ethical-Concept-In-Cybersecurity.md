## üá∫üá∏ A. U.S. Standpoint on Counterattacks

In the United States, counterattacking is **illegal** for private individuals or organizations.

### Laws that prohibit counterattacks:
- **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) ‚Äì 1986**  
- **Cybersecurity Information Sharing Act ‚Äì 2015**  
- Other federal cybercrime laws

### Why counterattacks are banned:
- Considered **vigilantism**  
- Could escalate attacks  
- Could damage innocent systems  
- Could provoke **state-sponsored actors**, creating international risks  

### Only allowed:
‚úî **U.S. federal government employees**  
‚úî **Approved military cybersecurity personnel**  

Everyone else must **defend only**.

---

## üåç B. International Standpoint on Counterattacks

The International Court of Justice (ICJ) allows counterattacks **only if all the following are true**:

1. The counterattack affects **only** the attacker  
2. It acts as a direct message to stop  
3. It does **not escalate** the conflict  
4. Its effects can be **reversed**

### Why organizations avoid counterattacks:
- These conditions are hard to guarantee  
- High uncertainty  
- Actions may cause further harm  
- Negative consequences are hard to control  

For deeper study, refer to the **Tallinn Manual** on international cyber warfare.

# üß© 3. Ethical Principles & How They Guide Decisions

Ethical frameworks like the **CIA Triad**, along with privacy laws and compliance requirements, guide ethical behavior.

---

# üîí A. Confidentiality (Ethical Aspect)

Confidentiality = only authorized users can access data.

Ethical confidentiality means:

- Respecting privacy  
- Protecting sensitive data (PII & SPII)  
- Never sharing data outside approved processes  
- Never granting unauthorized access  

### Violations may result in:
- Loss of reputation  
- Disciplinary action  
- Legal consequences  
- Harm to users or organizations  

---

# üõ°Ô∏è B. Privacy Protection

Privacy protection means safeguarding personal information from misuse.

### Key terms:
- **PII** (Personally Identifiable Information)  
  - Example: name, phone number  
- **SPII** (Sensitive PII)  
  - Example: SSN, passport number, credit card data  

SPII requires **stricter handling** because misuse leads to:

- Identity theft  
- Financial fraud  
- Legal violations  
- Personal harm  

### Ethical responsibilities:
- Identify vulnerabilities  
- Reduce organizational risk  
- Align security with business goals  
- Protect user data at all costs  

---

# ‚öñÔ∏è C. Laws & Legal Obligations

Laws define acceptable behavior for handling sensitive data.

### Ethical obligations:
- Perform your work honestly  
- Remain unbiased  
- Follow evidence-based decision-making  
- Respect local and international laws  
- Stay informed and competent  
- Protect people, systems, and infrastructure  

### Example: HIPAA  
A U.S. federal law protecting **PHI** (Protected Health Information).

Security professionals must ensure:
- Patient data is handled securely  
- Breaches are reported  
- PHI is never shared without consent
